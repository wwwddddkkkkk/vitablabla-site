Ask someone how long a piece of hard candy lasts and they will not say "about eleven minutes." They will say "about one meeting," or "roughly the drive to my mother's," or "the length of a bad conference call." Nobody times a sweet with a clock. We time it with the thing we were doing while we ate it — and, more strangely, we choose the sweet to fit the thing. There is a whole hidden system here, and almost nobody talks about it.

This is a piece about the snack as a timer: the quiet way people use food to measure and divide their day. The plain takeaway is that we don't only pick snacks by flavour — we pick them by duration, choosing something that will last exactly as long as the task we are trying to get through. A slow-melting coffee candy and a bag of crisps solve completely different problems, and the difference is not nutrition. It is time.

"We don't eat to fill a stomach. Half the time, we eat to fill an interval."

Every snack has a running time.

A crisp is over in a second. A bag of them is over in eleven minutes, whatever the bag says. A hard candy runs somewhere between five and fifteen minutes depending on whether you are the sort of person who bites. Chewing gum is the outlier — technically endless, which is part of why it feels like a habit rather than a treat and why gum has been quietly losing ground. Once you notice that every snack has a running time, you start to see people casting them like actors: this one lasts long enough for the queue, that one is over before the lift arrives.

We use snacks to make time feel shorter.

Waiting is the most uncomfortable thing a day can ask of you, and a snack is the cheapest available anaesthetic for it. Airport gates, waiting rooms, the last ten minutes of a call you are not speaking on — these are not hunger situations. They are duration situations. A snack gives the mouth a job so the mind stops checking the clock, which is why a slow-melting candy is disproportionately useful at an airport gate and a fast, loud snack is disproportionately useful when you want the time to pass all at once. The snack is not feeding you. It is metering the wait.

Slow snacks are for enduring; fast snacks are for punctuating.

The two categories do genuinely different work, and once you name them the choices get easier:

  • Slow snacks — hard candy, slow-melting coffee candy, a mug of anything hot. They accompany. You use them when you must stay in a situation: a long drive, a meeting, a queue, a night shift.
  • Fast snacks — crisps, crackers, a handful of crunchy fruit. They interrupt. You use them to break a task in half: the 3pm wall, the pause between two pieces of work, the moment you decide the email can wait ten minutes.
  • Endless snacks — gum, seeds, anything from a communal bowl. They have no ending, so they never mark anything, and the day flows past without a seam.

Most people already sort snacks this way without knowing it. The mistake is buying the wrong duration for the moment — a snack that ends too early leaves you sitting in the same wait with nothing to do, and one that lasts too long turns into a chore.

The best snacks announce an ending.

There is a reason a snack that finishes cleanly feels better than one that trails off. The end is the point. A small treat with a clear finish tells your body that the interval is closed and the next thing can start — it is a punctuation mark, not a paragraph. A cooling finish does this especially well, because it resets the mouth and leaves nothing behind, which is why the afternoon snack works better as a reset than a meal. A snack that ends is a snack that gives you your attention back.

What this means for what you keep in the drawer.

Stop stocking a snack drawer by flavour and start stocking it by duration. You need one slow thing for the meetings and the drives, one crunchy fast thing for the 3pm break, and, ideally, nothing endless at all. In practice that looks like a couple of slow-melting Frozili pieces in the coat pocket and a bag of crunchy freeze-dried fruit in the drawer — one that carries you through an hour, one that ends it. You are not managing hunger. You are managing time, and you have been doing it with sweets for years without ever saying so out loud.

Quick questions

Do people really choose snacks based on how long they last?

Often, yes — usually without noticing. People instinctively pick a slow-melting candy for a long drive or meeting and a quick, crunchy snack for a short break, because the snack is being used to fill or break an interval rather than to satisfy hunger.

What is a "slow" snack?

A slow snack is one that lasts as long as the situation you are in — hard candy, slow-melting coffee candy, or a hot drink. It accompanies a task you cannot leave, like a queue, a commute, or a long call, without demanding attention.

Why does a snack with a clean finish feel more satisfying?

Because the ending is what closes the interval. A snack that finishes cleanly — especially one with a cooling finish that resets the mouth — signals that the break is over and the next task can start, whereas an endless snack leaves the day without a seam.

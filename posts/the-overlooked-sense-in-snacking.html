Open a bag of coffee and watch what happens before anyone tastes anything. Someone leans in. A shoulder drops. The room agrees, wordlessly, that something good is about to happen. Nothing has been eaten yet — the whole event is happening in the nose. We talk endlessly about how snacks taste and, lately, how they crunch, but the sense that arrives first and sets the mood usually goes unnamed.

This is a piece about the role of smell in snacking, the most overlooked of the snack senses. The plain takeaway is that aroma does most of what we credit to flavour — the majority of what we call "taste" is actually smell — which means a snack's scent shapes our pleasure before the first bite lands. It's the missing sibling to the two sensory shifts the journal has tracked before, the sound of snacking and the idea that texture is the new flavour. Sound and texture got their moment; smell has been quietly doing the heavy lifting all along.

"You smell a snack before you taste it, and you've half-decided how you feel about it before it reaches your tongue."

Most of "taste" is actually smell.

Here is the fact that reframes the whole subject: most of what we experience as flavour is delivered by the nose, not the tongue. The tongue handles only the basic registers — sweet, salty, sour, bitter, savoury — while the nuance, the strawberry-ness of a strawberry or the roasted depth of coffee, arrives as aroma travelling up the back of the throat as we chew. This is why a snack tastes flat and cardboard-like when you have a blocked nose: the tongue is working fine, but the aroma channel is closed. Smell isn't a garnish on flavour. It's most of the meal.

Aroma is the first thing a snack does.

Smell is also the sense that arrives ahead of everything else. A snack's scent reaches you before the first bite and sets an expectation the taste then either confirms or breaks. The warm, roasted smell of coffee signals comfort and alertness before any caffeine is involved; the bright, sharp scent of cut fruit reads as fresh and clean before a single piece is crunched. Brands have always known this intuitively — bakeries vent their ovens toward the street for a reason — but as a snacker it's worth noticing how much of your anticipation is built in the few seconds between opening a packet and tasting what's inside.

Why scent is the strongest tie to memory.

Of all the senses, smell has the most direct line to memory and emotion, which is why a single whiff can drop you into a specific afternoon from years ago. The scent of a particular coffee can return you to a kitchen; the smell of strawberries can land you in a childhood summer. Snacks lean on this constantly. A familiar aroma doesn't just tell you what something is — it tells you how you felt last time, and quietly promises the same feeling again. That emotional shortcut is a large part of why certain smells feel like comfort rather than mere information.

What this means for a good snack.

If aroma does this much work, a few things follow for anyone choosing or making snacks:

  • Scent sets the mood: the smell on opening shapes your pleasure before flavour gets a turn.
  • Real beats fake: a genuine roasted-coffee or true-fruit aroma reads as quality; a chemical note reads as cheap.
  • Concentration helps: removing water concentrates a fruit's aroma, which is part of why freeze-dried fruit smells so vivid.
  • Smell carries memory: a familiar scent brings back a feeling, not just a flavour.
  • A clean finish matters: the smell a snack leaves behind is part of the experience, not an afterthought.

Once you start paying attention to it, smell turns out to be the sense quietly running the show. The roasted-coffee lift of a Frozili piece and the bright, concentrated scent of OhCrisp freeze-dried fruit are both doing their best work before you've finished the first bite — which is exactly the kind of detail that separates a considered snack from a forgettable one, and the through-line behind the case that modern snacks are getting more sensory.

Quick questions

Is taste really mostly smell?

Largely, yes. The tongue detects only the basic registers — sweet, salty, sour, bitter, and savoury — while most of the nuance we call flavour is aroma sensed through the nose as we chew. It's why food tastes flat and dull when your nose is blocked by a cold.

Why does smell trigger such strong memories?

Smell has an unusually direct connection to the brain's memory and emotion centres, more so than the other senses. That's why a single familiar scent — a certain coffee, ripe strawberries — can vividly return you to a specific time or place, often before you can even name what you're smelling.

Does aroma make a snack taste better?

It shapes the experience before you taste anything. A snack's scent sets your expectation and mood in the first seconds after opening, and a genuine, well-defined aroma — real roasted coffee or true fruit — reads as higher quality than a flat or chemical smell, which makes the whole snack more enjoyable.

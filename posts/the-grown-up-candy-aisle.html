Walk the candy aisle as an adult and notice what your hand does. It drifts past the cartoon mascots and the family multipacks and slows somewhere more deliberate: a sour gummy with a real-fruit claim, a single dark square, a tin of something cold and coffee-flavoured. You are not buying for a lunchbox. You are buying for yourself, on a Tuesday, with no occasion at all — and the aisle has quietly rearranged itself to meet you there.

This is an analysis of grown-up candy — why adults are not leaving the confectionery aisle but reshaping it, trading predictable chocolate bars for novel textures, tart flavours, and treats that feel chosen rather than childish. The plain takeaway: candy is a record-breaking, growing category precisely because adults now treat it as a small, deliberate pleasure, and the fastest movement is toward the unexpected. According to the National Confectioners Association's 2026 State of Treating report, U.S. confectionery sales hit a record $55 billion in 2025 and are forecast to reach $62.2 billion by 2030. This is the same shift that powers the modern snack swap: keep the treat, upgrade the object.

"Adults didn't grow out of candy. They grew particular about it — and the aisle grew up to keep them."

The aisle is growing, not shrinking.

The first thing to clear up is that candy is not in retreat. Confectionery reached a record $55 billion in U.S. sales in 2025, with the NCA reporting that 99.8% of households bought a confectionery product at least once that year. What is changing is not whether adults buy candy but how they justify it: people now eat chocolate and candy a few times a week in small, intentional amounts, treating a piece as a deliberate moment of joy rather than a guilty slip. The category is growing because permission, not restriction, is now the organising idea.

Non-chocolate is quietly taking share.

The clearest signal of a grown-up shift is in the mix. Non-chocolate candy grew from roughly one-third of U.S. confectionery sales in 2015 to 40.9% in 2025, according to the NCA's State of Treating report. That is the aisle reweighting toward exactly the formats adults reach for when they want something other than a familiar chocolate bar — sour, chewy, fruit-forward, and texturally interesting. Chocolate still leads at just over half the market, but the momentum is in the category that rewards novelty.

Texture and novelty are the new flavours.

Generation tells the story. The NCA finds that Boomers prefer traditional milk and dark chocolate, while Gen Z and Millennials over-index for gummy, chewy, and freeze-dried candies — choosing a treat as much for how it feels in the mouth as for how it tastes. This is the same instinct we have called texture as the new flavour: the snap, the chew, the dissolve. Younger adults are also the most likely to chase unexpected flavour combinations and to find them on TikTok, which is why freeze-dried candy — pure texture with concentrated taste — has moved from novelty to staple.

Nostalgia with a grown-up edge.

The last thread is memory. One-third of consumers, the NCA reports, are very interested in exact re-releases of nostalgic or retro candy — but the same shoppers want innovation alongside it. The grown-up aisle runs on both: the comfort of a remembered sweet and the curiosity of a format that didn't exist when you were ten. The winning grown-up treats tend to share a profile:

  • A real reason to say yes: a single-piece portion, a real-fruit ingredient, or a clear occasion.
  • An interesting texture: crunch, slow melt, or chew that a plain bar can't offer.
  • A flavour that isn't childish: coffee, tart berry, dark cocoa, something with an adult edge.
  • A format made for one: pocketable, tidy, and built for a personal moment rather than a party bowl.

Seen this way, grown-up candy is less a product type than a permission structure — pleasure with a defensible reason attached, which is the same logic behind permissible indulgence. It is the space both of our brands were built for: Frozili icy coffee candy as a grown-up coffee note with a cooling finish, and OhCrisp freeze-dried fruit as exactly the kind of real-fruit crunch the data says younger adults now reach for. The candy aisle didn't get smaller as we grew up. It got more interesting.

Quick questions

What is grown-up candy?

Grown-up candy is confectionery aimed at adults buying for themselves rather than for children — usually with a more sophisticated flavour, an interesting texture, a single-serve format, and a clear reason to enjoy it. Think coffee candy, tart freeze-dried fruit, or dark chocolate rather than cartoon-branded multipacks.

Is the candy market actually growing?

Yes. U.S. confectionery sales reached a record $55 billion in 2025 and are projected to hit $62.2 billion by 2030, according to the National Confectioners Association's 2026 State of Treating report, which also found 99.8% of households bought confectionery at least once that year.

Why do adults prefer gummy and freeze-dried candy now?

Largely for texture and novelty. The NCA reports that Gen Z and Millennials over-index for gummy, chewy, and freeze-dried candies and are more likely to seek unexpected flavours, while non-chocolate candy's share of the market grew from about one-third in 2015 to 40.9% in 2025 — evidence that adults increasingly choose treats for how they feel and surprise, not just how sweet they are.

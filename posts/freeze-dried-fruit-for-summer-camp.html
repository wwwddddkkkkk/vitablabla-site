A camp duffel is packed by an adult and unpacked by a child, and everything in between happens in a hot cabin with no refrigerator. The banana someone tucked in the side pocket on Sunday is a scientific event by Tuesday. The yogurt tube was never going to make it. What survives is whatever does not need cold, does not need a bowl, and does not mind being sat on — which is a surprisingly short list.

This is a case for OhCrisp freeze-dried fruit as a summer camp snack: freeze-drying removes about 95% of a fruit's water, so a strawberry becomes shelf-stable, crush-resistant when packed properly, and light enough that a week's worth barely registers in a bag. That is exactly the brief for a cabin shelf, a day-hike pocket, or a care package that has to travel by post in July heat.

"Camp food logic is simple: if it needs a fridge, a fork, or a flat surface, it will not be eaten."

Camp is the hardest test a fruit snack can face.

Fresh fruit fails at camp for four separate reasons at once: heat, bruising, mess, and time. Freeze-dried fruit is unbothered by all four, because water is what makes fruit heavy, perishable, and squashable, and freeze-drying takes almost all of it out. A bag of freeze-dried strawberries opened on day six tastes the same as it did on day one, which is not a claim any fresh fruit in a duffel can make. Real fruit crunch is fruit with the water removed and the flavour concentrated — nothing added to keep it stable.

It solves the "no fridge, no sink" problem specifically.

Cabins have shelves and hooks, not appliances. That rules out most of what parents actually want their children eating in a week of hot dogs and bug juice. Freeze-dried fruit is eaten dry from the bag with one hand, leaves no juice, no pit, no peel, and no sticky fingers to wash in a bathroom two buildings away. It is the same practical logic that makes it work for hiking, scaled to a week instead of an afternoon.

The camp uses worth packing for.

A few pouches cover more ground than their size suggests:

  • Cabin shelf snack — the between-activity handful that needs no fridge.
  • Day-hike pocket — light, unsquashable, no cleanup on a trail.
  • Breakfast fix — a scattering of freeze-dried berries over oatmeal or cereal in the mess hall.
  • Care package filler — it posts well in summer because there is nothing in it to melt.
  • Trading currency — colourful, unusual, and genuinely good, which matters more than adults expect.

Colour does real work in a homesick week.

Camp food is beige by design — it has to feed sixty children cheaply and safely. A pouch of bright pink strawberries or yellow mango is a small visual event in the middle of that, and children respond to how a snack looks long before they consider what is in it. This is not a nutrition argument; it is an argument about wanting to eat something. Colour is the reason a fruit snack gets chosen over the alternative, which we looked at more closely in colourful snacks and better routines.

Packing notes for a week away.

Freeze-dried fruit is crisp, which means it is brittle. Pack pouches upright along the side of a bag rather than under a sleeping bag, and send single-serve sizes rather than one large bag if the pouch will live on an open shelf — once opened, humidity is the only real enemy, and a resealed clip helps. A few pouches of OhCrisp tucked into a duffel is a quiet way to send something bright to a place with no fridge, no fruit bowl, and a great deal of sun.

Quick questions

Is freeze-dried fruit good for summer camp?

Yes. Freeze-dried fruit for summer camp works because it is shelf-stable without refrigeration, light enough to post or pack for a full week, and eaten dry with one hand — no juice, pits, peel, or cleanup in a cabin that has no fridge and no sink.

Does freeze-dried fruit melt or spoil in heat?

No. There is no chocolate, no coating, and almost no water in it, so heat does not melt it and it does not spoil the way fresh fruit does in a warm bag. Humidity after opening is the only thing to guard against, which a sealed or clipped pouch handles.

What snacks can you send in a camp care package?

Send things that survive the post in summer: freeze-dried fruit, dry crackers, and anything without chocolate or fresh produce. Single-serve pouches travel better than one large bag because they stay sealed until the day they are opened.

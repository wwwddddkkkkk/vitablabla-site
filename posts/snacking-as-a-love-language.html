Someone tips a bag of something good into a bowl, sets it in the middle of the table, and says nothing. People drift over. A meeting loosens; a road trip finds its rhythm; a quiet evening gets a small centre of gravity. Nobody calls it a gesture, but it is one — the oldest one there is, dressed down as a snack.

That instinct now has numbers behind it. The plain takeaway: snacking has quietly become a social act, a way people connect and show care rather than just refuel — and treating snacking as a love language is not a marketing line but something most consumers say out loud. In Mondelēz International's 2025 State of Snacking report, 71% of global consumers agreed that "sharing snacks with others is my love language," up eight points from the year before. It is the same impulse behind little-treat culture, turned outward: a small thing, offered to someone else.

"A shared snack is a low-stakes way to say I thought of you. It asks for nothing, interrupts nothing, and still lands as care."

What the survey actually says.

The headline figure is striking on its own, but the supporting numbers explain why it holds. Alongside the 71% who call sharing snacks a love language, Mondelēz found that 64% of consumers regularly snack to connect with others — also up eight points year over year — and that 93% agree they can always find a snack suitable for sharing. Read together, those three numbers describe a behaviour, not a slogan: snacks have become one of the easiest, most available tools people have for a small act of connection.

Why a snack is the perfect small gift.

A shared snack works because it is almost weightless as a social request. A full meal implies time and obligation; a gift implies an occasion. A snack implies neither — it can be accepted in a second or politely declined without anyone losing face. That low stakes is exactly what makes it travel so well between people who are not quite close enough for more. The bowl on the table, the "want one?" across a desk, the bag passed back through the car: each is connection sized down to something anyone can offer.

The shapes care takes.

Once you notice it, the shared snack appears in a dozen familiar forms. A few of the most common:

  • The communal bowl, set out with no announcement, that turns a room into a place people gather.
  • The offered piece, the single candy or square handed over mid-conversation as a tiny act of attention.
  • The board built to be picked at, where the whole point is that several hands reach in at once.
  • The road-trip bag, passed seat to seat, that keeps a long drive sociable.

Why brands are leaning in.

If snacking is increasingly about connection, the format matters as much as the flavour. Snacks designed to be shared — easy to pass, easy to portion, pleasant to set out — do more social work than ones built only for solo eating. That is part of why colourful, pick-at foods feel so at home on a table: a scatter of bright freeze-dried fruit on a board invites more hands than a sealed single serving ever could. The most shareable snacks are the ones that look generous before anyone has taken a piece.

None of this turns a snack into a substitute for real time with people — it is a supplement, a small and honest one. But the next time you tip something into a bowl, it is worth knowing the gesture is doing more than you think. Whether it is a board of OhCrisp freeze-dried fruit to pick at or a tin of Frozili coffee candy offered around a table, the snack is the easy part; the offering is the point. Quick questions

Is snacking really a love language?

For most consumers, yes — at least in how they describe it. In Mondelēz International's 2025 State of Snacking report, 71% of global consumers agreed that "sharing snacks with others is my love language," reflecting how often people use a shared snack as a small, everyday way to show care.

Why do people share snacks instead of meals to connect?

A snack is a low-stakes offer. It can be accepted in seconds or declined without awkwardness, and it requires no occasion or commitment, which makes it an easy way to create a moment of connection even between people who are not especially close.

What makes a snack good for sharing?

Shareable snacks are easy to pass, easy to portion, and pleasant to set out — think communal bowls, pick-at boards, and individually offered pieces. Colourful, pick-at foods work especially well because they look generous and invite several hands at once.

Close your eyes and bite something good. Before you've named the flavour, you've already heard the verdict: the dry tear of a crisp, the high snap of a cracker, the soft give of something past its best. We talk about snacks as things we taste, but a surprising amount of the pleasure arrives through the ears. The crunch is not the soundtrack to the snack. It is part of the snack.

This is a short look at the sound of snacking — why noise is doing more work in a bite than we give it credit for. The plain takeaway: people judge freshness, quality, and even flavour partly by the sound a food makes, which is why crunch is one of the most powerful tools a snack can have. It's the auditory half of the idea that texture is the new flavour: a snack you can hear feels more alive than one you can't.

"A silent crisp tastes stale even when it isn't. The ear decides freshness before the tongue gets a vote."

The experiment that proved you eat with your ears.

The clearest evidence comes from Oxford psychologist Charles Spence, whose 2004 "sonic chip" study had people eat Pringles while their own crunching was played back through headphones, electronically tweaked. When the crunch was made louder and higher-pitched, eaters rated the very same chips as around 15% fresher and crisper; when it was muffled, the chips seemed stale. The chip never changed — only the sound did. The work earned Spence an Ig Nobel Prize in 2008 and a lasting place in food science, because it showed that crispness is partly a thing we hear, not only a thing we feel.

Why the brain trusts a loud bite.

The link isn't arbitrary. In nature, a loud, sharp snap is a reliable signal of freshness: ripe produce and just-baked or just-fried foods are structurally crisp, and that crispness is what makes noise. A quiet, yielding bite usually means moisture has crept in and freshness has crept out. So the ear learned a useful shortcut — loud equals fresh, fresh equals good — and snacks that deliver a clean, audible snap get a small credibility bonus before the flavour even registers. It's a real reason fruit crunch can feel more satisfying than a chewy snack: the chew is silent, and silence reads as dull.

How snack brands are designing for noise.

Once you know sound carries meaning, you can build for it. Modern snacks increasingly engineer the bite as deliberately as the flavour, because a memorable noise is a form of quality you can't fake with a soft texture. A few of the ways sound shows up by design:

  • Structure over coating: airy, brittle architectures (freeze-dried, puffed, kettle-crisped) that fracture loudly instead of bending quietly.
  • Packaging cues: a sharp, rustly pack that primes the ear for a sharp, crisp product inside.
  • Pitch, not just volume: brands chase the high-frequency "snap" that the Spence work tied most strongly to perceived freshness.
  • Contrast bites: pairing a loud element with a soft one so the crunch stands out by comparison.

The quiet snacks that earn their silence.

None of this means louder is always better. Some of the best small snacks work precisely because they're silent — a slow-melting candy is supposed to dissolve without a sound, trading the crunch's quick thrill for a long, calm finish. The lesson isn't "make everything crunchy"; it's that sound is a design choice with meaning, so a snack should pick its register on purpose. OhCrisp freeze-dried fruit leans all the way into the loud, fresh snap; Frozili coffee candy does the opposite, choosing the quiet melt. Both are using sound deliberately — one to announce freshness, the other to signal calm.

Quick questions

Does the sound of a snack really affect how it tastes?

Yes. Research by Charles Spence found that making the crunch of a potato chip louder and higher-pitched led people to rate the same chip as fresher and crisper, while muffling the sound made it seem stale. The bite's noise shapes perceived freshness and quality before flavour is even considered.

Why do crunchy snacks feel more satisfying?

Because the brain treats a loud, sharp snap as a signal of freshness, learned from the fact that crisp foods are usually fresh and soft ones usually aren't. A snack you can hear reads as more alive and higher quality, so crunch delivers a small hit of satisfaction that silent, chewy textures don't.

Are quiet snacks worse than loud ones?

No — sound is a design choice, not a ranking. A slow-melting candy is meant to be silent, trading crunch for a long, calm finish, while a crisp snack uses noise to announce its freshness. The best snacks choose their register on purpose rather than always chasing volume.

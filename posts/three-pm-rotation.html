For one month, the studio kept a small whiteboard next to the snack drawer. Every time someone reached for something between 2:30 and 4:00 p.m., they made a tally mark. The board filled up faster than we expected. Patterns emerged. We are slightly embarrassed by them.

The data, in plain words.

  1. Frozili — 41%. By a lot. Almost always at 3:05.
  2. OhCrisp strawberry — 19%. The crunch was doing more work than expected.
  3. Almonds — 14%. Reliable. Slightly boring. Always there.
  4. Dark chocolate — 11%. Spiked on Mondays. Disappeared on Fridays.
  5. The salted plum jar — 8%. A specific person, a specific mood.
  6. Other / unknown — 7%. One person, three days, declined to tally.
"3:05 p.m. is, statistically, the loneliest minute of the workday."

What we read into it.

The 3 p.m. dip is real, it is consistent, and it has a preferred shape: something small, something that takes longer than ten seconds, something that doesn't require a fork. Frozili wins because it is all three. The crunch slot wins second because it sounds different from the rest of the afternoon, which feels, for some reason, like a reset.

What we changed in the studio.

We bought a second tin. The board now sits permanently next to the drawer. The salted plum jar is replaced as soon as it gets light; it has become a cultural artifact. We are still arguing about whether to chart 9 p.m.

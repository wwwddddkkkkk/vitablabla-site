"Healthy" has, over the last twenty years, been worn down to a sort of beige plastic. It used to mean something specific — usually about vegetables, sometimes about exercise. Now it appears on packaging that contains, by weight, mostly cane sugar. We have been trying not to use the word at all.

Here is what we say instead.

"We try to make snacks that don't owe you anything afterwards."

Three words we use.

  1. Honest. The label tells you the whole story. If there's only one ingredient, it says one ingredient, in plain English. If there's eleven, it says eleven, and you can read every one.
  2. Considered. Someone thought about the dose, the texture, the timing. A snack should not surprise you four hours later.
  3. Quiet. No spike, no crash, no marketing copy that requires a glossary. The snack does its job and gets out.

None of this is the same as "healthy." A square of dark chocolate is honest, considered, and quiet. So is a perfectly ripe apricot. So, for that matter, is a small piece of icy cool coffee candy that takes seven minutes to dissolve. None of them are wellness products. They are just snacks that don't owe you anything afterwards.

What we don't say.

We don't say "guilt-free." Guilt is not a nutrient. We don't say "clean." Food is not dirty. We don't say "superfood." Most foods are super, in the sense that they are alive or used to be.

What we hope happens.

You eat a small piece of Frozili, or a few pieces of OhCrisp, and you don't think about it again. There is no aftertaste of effort. There is no tally. There is just a small reset, or a bright crunch, and then the rest of your afternoon. That is the closest we can get to "better-for-you" without using a word that has stopped meaning anything.

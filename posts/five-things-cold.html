Cold makes flavor honest. It mutes the sweet, sharpens the acid, and stretches the finish. We learned most of what we know about candy by paying attention to which foods get better when they are cold, and why.

A short list, in no particular order, but with strong opinions.

1. Watermelon.

Warm watermelon is fine. Cold watermelon is a different fruit. The acid wakes up, the sweetness pulls back, the bite gets crisp. There is a reason every culture that grows them eats them this way.

2. Coffee.

This is not controversial anymore. But it took us a long time to admit that cold-brew is not a worse hot coffee — it's a better different beverage. The chocolate notes step forward, the acid rounds. We built Frozili on this premise.

3. Cucumber.

A cold cucumber on a hot day is, briefly, the best thing in the world. There is nothing fancy here. Just temperature.

4. Stone fruit.

Slightly chilled, not refrigerator-cold. There is a 30-minute window where a peach is at its absolute peak, and most people miss it because they forget about the bowl on the counter. We are no better at this than anyone else.

5. The one that surprised us.

Dark chocolate. Cold dark chocolate, eaten in slow shavings, tastes more chocolate-y than warm dark chocolate. We were prepared to hate this. A blind tasting in the studio settled the argument in about four minutes.

The pattern.

If a food has a strong, complex finish, cold is going to make it longer. If it has a one-note flavor, cold is going to be cruel to it. This is, more or less, our whole product philosophy.

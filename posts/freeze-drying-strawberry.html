Cut a strawberry in half. You will see, mostly, water — about 91% of it, by weight. The job of freeze-drying is to remove that water without removing the strawberry, and the trick to that is to never let the water be a liquid in the first place.

It sounds like a small distinction. It changes everything.

"The fruit doesn't dry. It just stops being wet."

The science, in two minutes.

The strawberry goes into a chamber and is frozen hard, all the way through. The chamber pressure is then dropped to a vacuum so deep that ice can no longer exist alongside liquid water at all. The only stable thing for a frozen water molecule to do, at that pressure, is leave the strawberry as vapor. This is called sublimation. The fruit doesn't dry. It just stops being wet.

What you get back is a shape that is structurally identical to the original — same cells, same pigments, same little yellow seeds — but mostly air. The first bite makes a sound that we can only describe as a polite snap.

Why color stays.

Most drying methods involve heat. Heat is bad for color. The pigments in fruit — anthocyanins, carotenoids, the oranges and reds and pinks we like — are temperature-sensitive. They oxidize, they fade, they shift from red toward brown. Freeze-drying never lets the strawberry get warm, so the color never gets the chance to leave.

The first time we pulled a tray of OhCrisp strawberries out of the dryer, we were quiet for about thirty seconds. They were almost too red.

Why flavor concentrates.

  1. Water leaves. Sugars, acids, and aromatics stay.
  2. Mass shrinks, density doesn't. A piece you could eat in two bites tastes like four berries.
  3. The crunch carries it. Texture changes how flavor is perceived. An airy crunch reads as bright.

It is, in our opinion, the most honest thing you can do to a piece of fruit. The fruit is still the fruit. We just got out of its way.

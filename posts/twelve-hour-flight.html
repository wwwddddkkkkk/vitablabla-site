A long flight is, fundamentally, a problem of pacing. Twelve hours is too long to sit still and too short to lose a real day to. The food situation oscillates between heavy meals you didn't ask for and bright lights you didn't want. We have flown a lot, and we have opinions.

"The job of an airplane snack is not to feed you. It is to give the day shape."

The three-bag system.

One opaque pouch, one rigid tin, one resealable zip bag. That's it. Anything bigger and you start eating out of obligation. The pouch is for crunch. The tin is for slow. The zip bag is for your seat-mate, in case they look at you long enough.

  1. Hour 1 — boarding. Nothing yet. You aren't hungry; you're stressed.
  2. Hour 2 — first dip. A pouch of OhCrisp, two pieces at a time, slowly. The crunch wakes you up without requiring a tray table.
  3. Hour 4 — meal service. Eat what you want; the snacks aren't for now.
  4. Hour 6 — the lull. A piece of Frozili. Seven minutes, icy, slow. Marks the middle of the flight in a way nothing else does.
  5. Hour 8 — second dip. The remaining OhCrisp, this time with intention. Drink water.
  6. Hour 10 — pre-landing fog. Another Frozili. The cold pulls you back.
  7. Hour 12 — descent. Pocket the rest. You'll want it in the customs line.

What we leave at home.

Anything that crumbs, anything that smells, anything that leaks if the cabin pressure changes its mind. Granola bars are surprisingly bad for this. Chocolate is fine until it isn't. Nuts are reliable but boring after hour four. Gum is a mistake on a long flight; chewing for that long disrupts your jaw.

One non-snack rule.

Drink twice as much water as you think. The job of an airplane snack is not to feed you. It is to give the day shape. The water is the actual meal.

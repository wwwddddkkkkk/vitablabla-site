This is the surprisingly specific job of coffee candy — especially the slow-melt kind. A piece of iced coffee candy gives your tongue something familiar (roast, sweetness, a little bitterness) and then finishes clean with a cooling edge. Why gum is the wrong tool: chewing reads like a decision.

Gum is great on walks. In meetings, it’s loud: jaw motion, minty perfume, the whole “I am actively chewing” broadcast. A slow melting candy is quieter. It sits in the background. It changes how your mouth feels without changing the room.

If you want the quick taxonomy, start with non-gum refresher after lunch and refreshing candy without mint. The shared idea is simple: “refreshing” can be cool and clean, not necessarily minty and sharp.

Iced coffee candy as a meeting ritual: pacing is the point.

Part of what people like about coffee flavored candy is that it has structure. Coffee already has bitterness, aroma, and an adult memory attached to it. When you slow it down, it becomes a small ritual instead of a sugar spike.

For the flavor logic, why iced coffee candy works explains the “iced” in plain language: cooling makes coffee feel like a finish. Not a new drink. Not a new problem.

Where it fits: before the meeting, during the meeting, after the meeting. Different jobs.

One piece can do three different things depending on timing.

  1. Before. Use it as a boundary: the cue that you’re switching from deep work into talk mode. If you like this idea, three p.m. rotation is the bigger picture.
  2. During. Use it quietly — a background flavor that keeps you grounded without chewing or crinkling wrappers.
  3. After. Use it as a clean finish, especially if your meeting involved lunch, coffee, or a lot of talking. Coffee breath freshening candy is the sister note.

A fruit tart is a small act of showing off. The pastry is blind-baked and cooled, the pastry cream piped smooth, and then comes the part everyone actually looks at: the fruit, arranged on top like tiles in a mosaic. It photographs beautifully for about an hour. Then the strawberries begin to weep, the kiwi slides, and the glaze that was meant to hold it all together turns the crisp shell soft from the inside.

Here is the plain takeaway: freeze-dried fruit for fruit tarts solves the one thing fresh fruit gets wrong — moisture — by adding intense, colourful fruit that never leaks into the cream or softens the crust. A scatter of OhCrisp freeze-dried fruit gives a tart concentrated berry flavour and real fruit crunch, and because it carries almost no water, it keeps the pastry crisp long after fresh slices would have gone glassy and grey.

"Fresh fruit makes a tart look good for an hour. Freeze-dried fruit makes it taste like the fruit for a day — no weeping, no sliding, no soggy shell."

The enemy of a fruit tart is water, and freeze-dried fruit has almost none.

Freeze-drying removes about 95% of a fruit's water while leaving its structure, colour, and flavour intact — which is exactly why it belongs on pastry. Fresh berries sit on pastry cream slowly releasing juice; that juice softens the crust and thins the cream until the whole thing loses its edges. Freeze-dried fruit brings the taste of ripe fruit with none of the leakage, so a tart holds its crisp shell and clean lines far longer than a classic fresh-fruit version.

Crushed into the cream, it flavours the whole tart, not just the top.

The trick most people miss is that freeze-dried fruit works in two places at once. Crushed to a powder and folded into the pastry cream or whipped mascarpone, it turns the filling pink or purple and pushes real strawberry or raspberry flavour all the way through — no watery pastel, just intense fruit. Then whole or halved pieces go on top for crunch and shape. One ingredient gives you a flavoured cream and a textured finish, which fresh fruit simply can't do.

The colour stays vivid because there's no water to dull it.

Anyone who has watched sliced strawberries fade to a dull red under glaze knows how quickly fresh fruit loses its shine. Freeze-dried fruit keeps a startling, saturated colour — the reds stay red, the raspberries stay almost neon — because the pigment isn't diluted or oxidising in juice. On a pale tart, that colour does a lot of the decorating for you, and it still looks bright at the end of a party rather than tired.

Ways to use it on a tart.

A few reliable moves, from simple to show-off:

  • Powder in the cream — blitz freeze-dried raspberries fine and fold into the pastry cream for colour and flavour throughout.
  • A crunch border — ring the edge with whole freeze-dried strawberries or blueberries for texture that holds all day.
  • A final snow — dust crushed fruit over the top just before serving for a matte, intensely fruity finish.
  • Mixed with fresh — a few fresh berries for gloss, freeze-dried for flavour and staying power.
  • On mini tarts for a crowd — one piece per bite-size shell, so nothing goes soggy on the platter.

Why it suits make-ahead and travel tarts especially.

If you're baking for a party, a picnic, or anything that has to survive a car ride, the water problem gets worse — a fresh-topped tart made in the morning is often past its best by the afternoon. Freeze-dried fruit is shelf-stable and stays crisp, so you can decorate ahead and still serve something that looks and tastes just-made. Quick questions

Can you use freeze-dried fruit on a fruit tart?

Yes, and it solves the tart's biggest problem. Freeze-dried fruit for fruit tarts adds concentrated flavour and crunch without releasing water, so the pastry stays crisp and the cream stays firm — unlike fresh fruit, which weeps juice and softens the shell within an hour or two.

Should I add freeze-dried fruit before or after baking?

After. Add it once the tart is assembled and cooled — either crushed into the pastry cream or scattered on top. Adding it before baking exposes it to heat and moisture, which defeats the purpose of its crunch and vivid colour.

Will freeze-dried fruit go soggy on top of pastry cream?

Only slowly. Because it contains so little water and sits on top rather than in the cream, freeze-dried fruit stays crisp far longer than fresh fruit. For the crunchiest result, scatter it on shortly before serving; for flavour that lasts all day, crush some into the cream as well.

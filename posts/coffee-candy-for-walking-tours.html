Guided walks are built around stopping, not moving.

The physical demand of a walking tour is misread almost every time. You cover perhaps two miles in three hours, which is a stroll — but you spend most of that time standing still in one spot, outdoors, talking and listening. Standing in warm air with your mouth open is what dries a throat, not distance. Coffee candy for walking tours works because it is designed for exactly that shape of time: it lasts through a whole stop rather than a single bite, and it asks nothing of you while the guide is speaking.

You cannot drink coffee on a tour, and you usually cannot buy one either.

The mid-tour energy dip is real and it lands somewhere around the ninety-minute mark, which is almost always the point at which the route is furthest from anywhere selling anything. Even when there is a café, joining a queue means losing the group. A slow-melting candy is the only version of a coffee ritual that survives a moving itinerary: no cup, no lid, no cooling window, nothing to carry into a chapel where drinks are not allowed. It gives you the roasted flavour and cooling finish of the pause without requiring the pause itself.

Quiet is part of the point.

There used to be a rack at every checkout, a little wall of foil and mint at elbow height, engineered for the unthinking grab. You can still find it, but look closely and it has thinned: fewer facings, more space given to gummies and protein bars, the gum hanging on like a shop that forgot to update its sign. Somewhere in the last decade, one of the most reliable impulse buys in the world quietly stopped being reliable.

This is a short analysis of the decline of chewing gum — what happened to a category that once seemed permanent, and what is taking its place. The plain takeaway: U.S. chewing gum unit sales fell about 32% between 2018 and 2023, according to market researcher Circana, and the slump points to a deeper shift in how people want to refresh themselves. Part of the answer is the rise of quieter, calmer alternatives like a non-gum refresher after lunch.

"Gum did not lose to a better gum. It lost to the slow realisation that nobody especially enjoyed chewing it."

The numbers, and when the bubble burst.

The break point was the pandemic. The number of packages of gum sold in the United States dropped by nearly a third in 2020, as masks made bad breath a private problem and the checkout impulse buy evaporated, according to Circana. Demand never fully came back: U.S. gum sales reached only about 1.2 billion units in 2023, still 32% below the 2018 figure. The trend is global, too — Euromonitor put worldwide gum sales above $16 billion in 2023, roughly 10% under their 2018 level. Dollar sales have crept back toward pre-pandemic numbers, but mostly because the average pack got more expensive, not because more people are chewing.

Why people stopped chewing.

The decline is less about one cause than several pulling the same direction. Bad breath stopped being a public anxiety during years of masking; the impulse rack lost foot traffic to online and delivery shopping; and a growing share of consumers began avoiding both sugar and artificial sweeteners, which left even sugar-free gum looking suspect. Generational taste matters most of all: market researchers note that Gen Z leans toward novelty candies like sour gummies rather than gum, so the cohort that should be refreshing the category is largely skipping it. The big players noticed — Mondelez sold off its gum brands, including Trident and Dentyne, in 2022 to focus on faster-growing snacks.

What is filling the gap.

When a near-universal habit fades, the need it served does not vanish; it migrates. People still want a small mouth reset, a between-tasks pause, and something to do with a restless minute. Those jobs are being picked up by a scattered set of alternatives:

  • Mints and lozenges for the breath-freshening job, minus the chewing and the litter.
  • Sour gummies and novelty candy for the impulse-treat and flavour-hit job, especially among younger buyers.
  • Functional and "wellness" formats as brands reposition refreshment around focus and calm rather than fresh breath.
  • Slow-melting hard candies for the quiet, hands-free pause — a reset you let dissolve instead of working with your jaw.

The deeper shift: from chewing to dissolving.

Read together, the data describes a change in register, not just a fallen category. Gum was busy and a little anxious — visible jaw, disposable wad, a habit built on hiding a flaw. The formats gaining ground are calmer and cleaner, designed to be enjoyed rather than worked. That is the space a grown-up alternative to basic mints lives in, and where a slow-melting coffee piece like Frozili fits: it refreshes without chewing, leaves nothing to spit out, and treats the small reset as a pleasure instead of a cover-up. Gum's long decline may simply be the sound of people deciding they would rather let something melt than chew it.

Quick questions

Is chewing gum really in decline?

Yes. U.S. chewing gum unit sales fell about 32% between 2018 and 2023, after dropping nearly a third in 2020 alone, according to Circana. Global volumes are down too, and dollar sales have recovered mainly because packs cost more, not because more people are chewing.

Why are people chewing less gum?

Several forces overlap: masking during the pandemic made bad breath a private worry, impulse-buy foot traffic moved online, and many consumers now avoid both sugar and artificial sweeteners. Younger buyers also favour novelty candies like sour gummies over gum, so the category is not being replenished by the next generation.

What are people choosing instead of gum?

The job gum did — a quick mouth reset and a small pause — is migrating to mints and lozenges, sour and novelty candies, functional formats sold around focus and calm, and slow-melting hard candies. Many of these are quieter and litter-free, suggesting people increasingly prefer something that dissolves to something they have to chew.

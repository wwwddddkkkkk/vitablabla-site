There is a particular kind of person who scoffs at the idea of a snack drawer. The phrase sounds infantile. It evokes a plastic tray of granola bars at a chain hotel. They would rather be the person who simply does not snack — eats lunch, eats dinner, holds the line.

We were that person. We are no longer that person. The dip at three in the afternoon is not a moral failing; it is the body doing math. You can either fight it or you can plan for it. Planning is cheaper.

The rules of a good drawer.

  1. Six slots, not twelve. A drawer with too many options becomes a graveyard.
  2. Three textures, minimum. Crunchy, slow, soft. Boredom is a flavor.
  3. Nothing that requires a fork. The whole point is friction-less.
  4. One thing for the dip. Specifically the 3 p.m. one.
  5. One thing for the late one. The 9 p.m. one is sneakier.
  6. Replace what you eat. A drawer that empties is a drawer that gets ignored.
"A drawer with too many options becomes a graveyard."

What's in ours, this week.

For the record, and with the slightly self-conscious awareness that putting our own products on the list reads like a sales pitch: we keep a tin of Frozili for the slow reset, a pouch of OhCrisp for crunch, and four civilian items — almonds, dark chocolate, a small jar of olives, and the stubbornly unkillable salted plum.

The hardest slot.

The dip slot is the hard one. Most snacks are too much — they want to be a meal, or they spike you and drop you. We built Frozili partly to fill it. A small piece of icy cool coffee candy. Seven minutes, slow on purpose, no real lift, just a comma in the day.

If you have a better candidate for that slot, please write to us. We are always rotating.

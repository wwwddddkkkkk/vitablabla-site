The first time it happens it is an accident. A pouch of freeze-dried strawberries has been carried around in a bag for a week, and when you open it there is a drift of fine pink dust in the bottom corner — the sugar-bright colour of a chalk pastel, and somehow more intensely strawberry than the strawberries themselves. Most people brush it into the bin. That dust is the most useful ingredient in the pouch.

This is a short, practical piece on freeze-dried fruit powder: what it is, how to make it in about a minute, and what to do with it. The plain takeaway is that freeze-dried fruit powder is simply freeze-dried fruit blended to a fine powder — nothing added — and because it carries real fruit flavour and colour with almost no water, it can flavour frosting, yogurt, or a milk drink without thinning them the way fresh fruit does. It is the closest thing a home kitchen has to a natural flavour concentrate.

"Fresh fruit gives colour and takes texture. Fruit powder gives colour and takes nothing."

What freeze-dried fruit powder actually is.

Freeze-dried fruit powder is real fruit that has had its water removed by freeze-drying and then been ground fine — usually one ingredient, no sugar, no colouring. Because roughly all of the water is gone, a spoonful of powder carries the flavour of far more fruit than a spoonful of the fresh version, and it dissolves rather than sits. That is why a teaspoon of raspberry powder will turn a whole bowl of buttercream pink and sour, while a raspberry mashed into the same bowl mostly makes it wet.

How to make it in one minute.

You need a pouch of freeze-dried fruit and any small blender, spice grinder, or even a bag and a rolling pin.

  1. Start dry. Use fruit straight from a freshly opened pouch — it must be crisp, not bendy. Any softness means it has already taken on moisture and it will clump instead of powdering.
  2. Blitz in short bursts. Ten to twenty seconds in a spice grinder or high-speed blender. Longer does not make it finer; it just heats the powder.
  3. Sieve if you care. Push it through a tea strainer to catch seeds — strawberry and raspberry are the two that need it.
  4. Store it airtight, immediately. Powder pulls in humidity much faster than whole pieces, so a small screw-top jar beats a folded bag. The same logic applies to storing freeze-dried fruit after opening.

Where it works better than fresh fruit.

Fruit powder earns its place anywhere water would ruin the texture. Whipped cream, buttercream, cream cheese frosting, white chocolate, meringue, and butter all break, weep, or seize when you introduce fresh fruit; powder flavours them without changing the ratio of anything. Stir a teaspoon of mango powder into a jar of yogurt and you get colour and sharpness with no puddle; stir in a chopped mango and you get a bowl of fruit swimming in thinned yogurt. It also makes a rather good rim for a glass, mixed half-and-half with sugar.

The other half of the pouch.

You do not need to powder the whole bag, and you shouldn't. The pleasure of real fruit crunch is the reason to buy the fruit in the first place, and it is the thing the powder cannot give you back. The sensible ratio is most of the pouch eaten whole or scattered on things, and a handful turned to powder when you need flavour that dissolves. Powder is a technique; crunch is the point.

A short list of things to dust it on.

  • Iced lattes and hot chocolate — strawberry or raspberry powder on the foam, in place of cocoa.
  • Frosting and whipped cream — one to two teaspoons per cup, added at the end.
  • Yogurt, cottage cheese, and porridge — colour and flavour without loosening the bowl.
  • Popcorn or buttered toast — powder plus a little sugar sticks to fat beautifully.
  • The rim of a summer drink — mixed with caster sugar on a wet glass.

Keep a jar of it beside the salt and you will find yourself reaching for it more than you expect. A pouch of OhCrisp is two ingredients pretending to be one: something to eat with your fingers, and something to make everything else taste like fruit.

Quick questions

How do you make freeze-dried fruit powder at home?

Blitz crisp freeze-dried fruit in a spice grinder or small blender for ten to twenty seconds, sieve out any seeds, and store it airtight straight away. No other ingredients are needed — the powder is simply the fruit, ground.

What can you use freeze-dried fruit powder for?

Use it wherever water would spoil the texture: frosting, whipped cream, white chocolate, yogurt, porridge, latte foam, popcorn, or a sugared glass rim. It adds real fruit flavour and colour without adding moisture.

How long does homemade fruit powder keep?

Kept in a sealed, airtight jar away from heat and light, it stays usable for months, but it absorbs humidity much faster than whole freeze-dried pieces. If it clumps or turns sticky, the moisture has got in and the flavour will fade.

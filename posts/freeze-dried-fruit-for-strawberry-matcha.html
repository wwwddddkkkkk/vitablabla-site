The drink arrived in layers, the way it does in every photograph of it: pale pink at the bottom, a band of milk, then that startling green sitting on top like moss on stone. It is a beautiful thing and, most of the time, a slightly disappointing one. The strawberry layer is usually syrup — sweet, jammy, and about as fruit-adjacent as a scented candle. The matcha is the only part of the glass with an opinion. The fruit is just there for colour.

This is a note on using freeze-dried fruit for strawberry matcha — the small swap that makes a café drink taste like fruit instead of sugar. The plain takeaway is that crushed freeze-dried strawberry gives a strawberry matcha latte real fruit flavour and colour without adding syrup, because freeze-drying removes the water and leaves the fruit concentrated. You are not sweetening the drink. You are actually putting strawberries in it.

"Syrup makes a drink pink. Fruit makes it taste like something."

Freeze-dried strawberry is basically strawberry, minus the water.

Freeze-drying removes roughly all of the moisture from a piece of fruit while leaving its structure, colour, and flavour compounds behind — which is why a freeze-dried strawberry tastes more intensely of strawberry than a fresh one does. In a drink, that matters twice over. Fresh strawberry would water down a latte and turn it pale and thin; syrup would sweeten it and flatten the matcha. A spoonful of crushed freeze-dried strawberry does neither. It dissolves into the milk as concentrated fruit, and it is the reason freeze-dried fruit works in drinks generally.

It leaves the matcha alone.

The whole problem with a syrupy strawberry matcha is that sugar and matcha are fighting for the same space. Matcha is grassy, vegetal, and gently bitter — a flavour with edges — and a heavy syrup rounds those edges off until you are drinking a green milkshake. Crushed freeze-dried fruit brings acidity and aroma rather than sweetness, so the two flavours sit next to each other instead of on top of each other. The strawberry stays bright; the matcha keeps its bitterness. That contrast is the drink people think they are ordering.

How to make one at home.

This takes about three minutes and no equipment beyond a spoon and something to crush with. The order matters more than the technique:

  1. Crush the fruit first. Take 4–6 freeze-dried strawberry pieces and crush them into a rough powder — a spoon against the side of a mug is enough.
  2. Make a paste, not a slurry. Add a splash of milk to the powder and stir until it turns into a thick pink paste. This stops it clumping later.
  3. Fill with cold milk and ice. Loosen the paste into the milk, then add the ice. You want the pink layer settled before the green arrives.
  4. Float the matcha last. Whisk the matcha separately and pour it gently over the back of a spoon. It sits on top; that is the entire visual trick.
  5. Scatter a little more fruit on the surface. A few crushed flakes on the foam give you crunch and aroma before the first sip.

The same trick works across the drinks menu.

Once you have crushed freeze-dried fruit in the cupboard, it stops being a matcha-specific idea. Raspberry into a cold-brew soda. Mango into a lassi. Strawberry into a plain glass of milk for someone small who will be delighted. It behaves the way a spice does — a dry, intense thing you add a little of — rather than the way fresh fruit does. Because it is shelf-stable, it also does not rot in the back of a fridge while waiting for you to have a good idea, which is more than can be said for the punnet of strawberries most of us have quietly composted.

Why this counts as a better version, not just a prettier one.

A strawberry matcha made with real fruit is sharper, less sweet, and more honest about what it is. You control the sugar, because you are adding fruit and not syrup, and the colour that shows up in the glass is the actual colour of the strawberry rather than a dye. That is the small argument behind OhCrisp: real fruit crunch is not only for bowls and boards — crushed into a paste, it can quietly fix a drink that has been getting by on looks. Make it once and the café version starts to taste like a photograph of itself.

Quick questions

Can you put freeze-dried fruit in a matcha latte?

Yes. Crush freeze-dried strawberries into a powder, mix them with a splash of milk to form a paste, then build the drink on top. The fruit dissolves into the milk and gives real strawberry flavour and colour without syrup.

Is freeze-dried strawberry better than strawberry syrup in a drink?

It gives you fruit rather than sugar. Syrup mainly adds sweetness and colour, while crushed freeze-dried strawberry adds concentrated fruit flavour and acidity, which leaves the matcha's grassy bitterness intact instead of burying it.

How much freeze-dried fruit do I need for one drink?

Four to six pieces of freeze-dried strawberry, crushed, is enough for a standard glass. The fruit is concentrated, so a small amount flavours the whole drink — add more only if you want a deeper pink.

Will it go soggy or clump?

Only if you drop it straight into cold milk. Crushing it first and making a paste with a little milk prevents clumping, and any fruit you scatter on top should go on last so it stays crisp.

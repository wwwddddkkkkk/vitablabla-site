There is a rhythm to a working day that almost nobody admits to. You sit down. You answer two emails. You stand up. You make a coffee you don't really need. You sit down again. And around three in the afternoon, something quietly slips — a small dip in attention, a small dip in mood, a small dip in the willingness to do whatever the next hard thing is.

The question we kept asking ourselves at the studio was: what's the smallest possible thing that can mark the end of that dip? Not a long break. Not a meal. Not even a coffee. Something more like a comma than a period.

"We wanted a snack that took up the same physical space as a breath."

That sentence ended up taped to the wall above our R&D bench. It is a slightly precious thing to write on a wall, but it has been useful. It is the brief, more or less, for Frozili.

A short history of slow snacks.

People have been using small, slow foods to mark the texture of the day for a very long time. Mints after meals. Sucking sweets on long car rides. Lozenges in winter, gum at airport gates, a square of dark chocolate at the end of a meeting. None of these are about nutrition, exactly — they are about pacing.

Most modern snacks have moved in the opposite direction. They are designed to be efficient: fast, full, calorie-dense, eat-and-go. There is nothing wrong with that. But it leaves a gap. There is no efficient snack that helps you slow down.

Why coffee, why icy.

Coffee is a flavor with strong associative weight. Almost everyone has a memory attached to it, and most of those memories involve a small pause: the walk to the café, the first sip, the moment before you start working again. We wanted that pause without the caffeine, without the cup, without the wait.

The icy part was less obvious. It came from a string of taste tests where we kept noticing that cold finishes felt longer than warm ones. A mint, a frozen grape, a piece of cold watermelon — all of them lingered in a way that felt out of proportion to their size. Cold, it turned out, is a kind of time stretcher.

"Cold, it turned out, is a kind of time stretcher."

How we built it.

Frozili is a hard candy with a slow-release cooling layer at its center. The outside is built around a cold-brew coffee reduction; the inside uses a small amount of natural cooling agents to extend the icy sensation past the moment the candy is gone. We tested forty-three formulations to get there, mostly with our friends and a small group of people in their twenties and thirties who agreed to chew thoughtfully and tell us what they noticed.

Our three rules, in order:

  1. It has to last. Below seven minutes and the ritual evaporates.
  2. It has to feel cold. Not menthol-cold. River-cold. Spring-cold.
  3. It has to taste like coffee. Not coffee-flavored. Coffee.

What it's for.

People reach for Frozili before a meeting, between two long emails, on a long flight, halfway through a study session. It is a small reset, not a meal. We like that. We think more snacks should be small resets.

If you've been reading the journal for a while, you already know we are slightly obsessive about the difference between something you eat and something you experience. Frozili is, hopefully, on the experience side.
